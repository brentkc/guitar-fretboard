Anatomy of the Guitar

Learn the parts of the guitar before you play a single note

Main Parts

  • Headstock â€” the top of the neck where the tuning pegs live. Turning a peg tightens or loosens a string, raising or lowering its pitch.
  • Nut â€” a small slotted piece between headstock and neck that keeps the strings at the right spacing and height.
  • Neck â€” the long wooden shaft you wrap your fretting hand around.
  • Fretboard (fingerboard) â€” the flat face of the neck with metal frets embedded across it. Pressing a string behind a fret shortens its vibrating length, raising its pitch.
  • Frets â€” the metal bars. Each fret = one semitone. Moving one fret up raises pitch by a half step.
  • Body â€” the large hollow (acoustic) or solid (electric) section that the neck is attached to.
  • Soundhole / Pickups â€” acoustic guitars project through the soundhole; electric guitars use magnetic pickups to convert vibration to an electrical signal.
  • Bridge & Saddle â€” the strings anchor here at the body end. Saddle height affects action (string height above frets).

How to Hold & Tune Your Guitar

Good posture and being in tune are essential from day one

Posture

  • Sit up straight; rest the guitar's waist on your dominant-side thigh (or the left thigh in classical style).
  • The neck should angle slightly upward â€” not pointing at the floor.
  • Your fretting arm elbow should hang freely; don't grip the neck with your palm.
  • Curve your fretting fingers so the tips press the strings, not the pads.
  • Keep your thumb roughly behind your middle finger on the back of the neck.

Strumming Patterns

Rhythm is what makes chord changes feel like music

The Basics: D = Down, U = Up

Hold a pick between your thumb and index finger. Relax your wrist â€” strumming comes mostly from the wrist, not the arm. Keep the motion fluid and consistent.

Pattern 1 â€” All Downs (4/4 time)

Beat: 1 2 3 4 D D D D Count: ONE TWO THREE FOUR

Use this on every chord until your changes are locked in. Set a metronome at 60 BPM. Slow = good.

Pattern 2 â€” Down/Up (feel the "and")

Beat: 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & D U D U D U D U

When you strum up, not every string needs to sound â€” a light brush of the top 3â€“4 strings is fine and sounds natural.

Pattern 3 â€” Folk/Pop Pattern

Beat: 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & D D U U D U

This is one of the most common patterns in pop, folk, and country. Count out loud: "ONE, two-AND, four-AND, FOUR-AND".

Pattern 4 â€” Reggae "skank" (off-beats only)

Beat: 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & U U U U

Mute the strings slightly with your strumming hand on the beat, and let the up-stroke ring. Creates that classic reggae chop.

Tip: Keep your strumming arm moving even when you're not hitting strings (air strums). This keeps your timing locked and makes missed beats sound intentional.
